## Scope and phenotype

- **Scope:** NAGS gene — variant interpretation criteria for N-acetylglutamate synthase deficiency, under the ClinGen Urea Cycle Disorders Expert Panel Specifications to the ACMG/AMP guidelines.[^1]
- **Phenotype:** N-acetylglutamate synthase deficiency (NAGSD; OMIM #237310) is an autosomal recessive urea cycle disorder caused by deficiency of NAGS, the enzyme that produces N-acetylglutamate (NAG), an essential allosteric activator of CPS1; it is the only urea cycle disorder treatable with a single drug (N-carbamylglutamate).[^4]

## Recent ClinVar activity

ClinVar data for NAGS is sparse, consistent with the rarity of NAGSD. Approximately 21 disease-associated mutations have been reported in the literature, including coding variants, splicing variants, and non-coding regulatory variants in the NAGS enhancer and first intron.[^5] The small variant pool means VUS resolution depends heavily on functional assay evidence and case-level data rather than population frequency. Non-coding variants in NAGS regulatory regions are a recognized diagnostic challenge for this gene.[^6]

## Key literature

1. **Caldovic L et al. (2018)** — Documents a recurrent pathogenic variant in the NAGS enhancer region causing NAGSD through reduced HNF1 binding; highlights the importance of non-coding variant assessment for this gene.[^7]

2. **Enns GM et al. (2021)** — Characterizes novel non-coding sequence variants in the NAGS first intron defining a regulatory element; directly relevant to PS3/BS3 and PS1/PM5 criteria for regulatory variants.[^8]

3. **Abou Haidar E et al. (2023)** — Case series of partial NAGS deficiency presenting as postpartum hyperammonemia; illustrates late-onset phenotype and the diagnostic value of NCG treatment response as functional evidence.[^9]
